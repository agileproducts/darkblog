# What is product management?

There is a lot of nonsense written about product management. "You are the CEO of the product", is one once popular definition. Not, in my experience, in any organisation that has an actual CEO. Another variation has that a product manager sits at the intersection of technology, user experience design and business - a set of LinkedIn skills rather than a role. To be responsible for proving the 'value' and 'viability' of a product is another, which seems to fit only in a situation where you get to decide whether the product should exist in the first place, as opposed to just navigating the constraints of a business your employer has long since decided they want to be in.

One way to define the job of a product manager within an organisation is  - you take the decisions as to what product features to build at what time. Therefore by implication you also take the decisions as to what not to build. And you take responsiblity for those decisions.

The typical situation in an enterprise or organisation is that there are always far more things you should or could do with a particular product than you have the time or resources to accomplish. All of these things will probably offer value to somebody, whether to particular types of customer or to colleagues ('stakeholders') with their own objectives to achieve and their own ideas about how your product can help. Sometimes those ideas will be great, but expensive. Sometimes their value will appear questionable to everyone else. Sometimes they'll contradict each other. Sometimes they'll be at odds with the vision for the product you are building or the organisation's overall strategy which informs it. Most often, they'll be great ideas and you'd love to say yes, _but you just don't have the resources to say anything more positive than 'not yet'_.

It's not at all uncommon for people to believe the way to deal with this is that all these ideas and feature requests should be collated in some kind of giant spreadsheet. Perhaps even with effort estimates and projected shipping date. Or that they should simply 'raise tickets' and wait for their wish to be executed, like ordering a new mouse from the IT helpdesk. This is a fundamental misconception as to the nature of product management. A giant list of features does not a coherent product make. You can't please everybody, or satisfy every single potential customer, and it is futile and self-defeating to try. Nor can you spend years chewing through a giant to-do list while the world moves on. Imagine at the time of writing being two years into a three-year backlog of Stuff while ignoring the AI revolution. Creating giant feature lists is not a strategy, it's the absence of a strategy. Product management is about _making choices_.

It's also, importantly, about owning those choices. Sometimes you may get the sense that colleagues question why _you_ get to make these decisions, at whatever level of product responsibility you hold. Why isn't a collective decision from the development team, or a vote amongst the stakeholders? It's not because the product manager is some kind of lonely autocrat possessed of a superior wisdom. The best decisions are informed by partnership - with business owners, with engineering leads, or with input from senior leaders. But somebody has to be accountable for those decisions, and be willing to field the brickbats if they turn out poorly. Decisions cannot be made by committees because committees are the absence of accountability.

Product management is about making choices, and taking responsibility for those choices.
