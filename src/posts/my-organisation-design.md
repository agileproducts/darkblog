# My design for an organisation

If I were asked to design a small to midsize organisation to develop digital products, it would look like this.

First of all, everyone would report to a single person on the executive board. I'll call this person 'CTO' because it's the title that resonates most with me, but it doesn't matter what the exact title is. The important part is - there is one responsible leader. The company where I work splits 'product' and 'technology' right up to this level and it leads to untold problems with accountability and decision making. Many of our other problems are downstream of this. When it comes to product development and delivery, the buck stops with my CTO. They are not though responsible for the IT helpdesk or the office software or the WiFi. This is basic infrastructure and sits with the COO.

Below the executive, there is a leader for each of the three core disciplines - Engineering, Product, UX Design. Something British in me always irks at the 'VP' job title, but it's inescapable, so we'll call these roles that. At this level I'd also have an Operations Director with a small team to manage overall budgeting, planning and reporting. This is an enabling function which means that engineering leaders don't spend their time doing budget spreadsheets, it should not become a gatekeeping bureaucracy or old-school project management office.

Below this level, I'd have divisions/areas corresponding to my main products or services. These would be led by a Principal Engineering Manager and a Product Director. Perhaps it's a cop-out not to nominate a single leader here, but my observation is that these setups either work as a partnership or one person emerges as the de-facto leader. Let's not formalise it until we need to. Each of these divisions would round out their leadership team with a strong design lead and a good programme manager. This might also be a good place to hang distinguished individual contributors who help make everything work.

Within these divisions, the unit of delivery is the classic two-pizza product team - an engineering lead and their developers/qa, product manager, designer. Project managers appear on an ad-hoc basis, spanning multiple teams where complexity or coordination requires it. 

I'm a big fan of the engineering manager role which line manages the developers in this setup, but I'm wary of creating a setup where those roles limit my flexibility to restructure teams. Similarly with product management - in general where you only expect one of something per team, it's best to run lean and not overhire. I used to be a business analyst and owe so much to that role, but I think at least for this level I'm not hiring for that. Those functions belong to product or project management.

I think it's very important to have a career track where strong individual contributors can grow without having to become managers and become something like Principal Engineers/Designers/Product. These people are my go-to when new or challenging or hard-to-place work comes in, otherwise they help teams where needed. I don't like job titles like 'Architect'. What does an architect do that my most senior engineers don't?

A few enablers are required, reporting in to the relevant VPs. Engineering probably needs some specialists in security, infrastructure, databases. I'm reluctant to create too many roles like this though, in my experience they risk becoming shadow distinguished IC spots attracting some of the best people away from the main purpose of the organisation. I don't believe in specialised 'front end' and 'back end' engineering roles in teams, if we need one or two real experts in front-end tooling etc, they can go here. 

To a first approximation, product managers should analyse and present their own data and not farm it off to an analyst team - AI makes this easier than ever to learn and do. However some specialist data analysis capability is probably useful, reporting to the product leadership. Similarly I'm entirely unconvinced by the trend for UX Design to splinter into multiple narrow roles. Designers working in teams should be all-rounders, with a small amount of central support from people specialised in visual design or research techniques.

The key principle behind the whole structure is that the teams who do the work are the most important element, that it remains one organisation with one leader, and that overheads and enablers are kept to a minimum. It should be possible for good people to enjoy successful careers as managers or ICs while remaining focused on the core mission.
